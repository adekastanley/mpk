1. Executive Summary
This document defines the requirements for the official website of My Period Companion. The website is not intended to replace the core application channels; rather, it will serve as the public gateway and credibility layer for the product ecosystem. It will explain the mission, communicate the value proposition, support user onboarding, provide trusted menstrual health education, showcase accessibility and inclusion commitments, and create clear pathways for partners, funders, schools, clinics, NGOs, and adolescent users to engage safely with the initiative.
The source application requirement positions My Period Companion as an AI-powered, voice-first, disability-inclusive menstrual health ecosystem integrating education, period tracking, rights advocacy, product access, emotional support, and physical-first touchpoints such as the First Period Toolkit. The official website must therefore reflect the same principles: simple language, privacy-first design, disability inclusion, multi-language readiness, safety escalation awareness, and practical calls to action.
Website positioning statement
The official website should make My Period Companion easy to understand, easy to trust, easy to access, and easy to partner with. It should be warm, safe, adolescent-friendly, culturally sensitive, and credible enough for schools, funders, clinics, NGOs, and policymakers.

1.1 Expected Website Outcomes
Improve public understanding of the application and the menstrual health problem it solves.
Provide a professional official presence that can be shared with funders, partners, schools, community organizations, and media.
Give users clear access routes to WhatsApp onboarding, IVR, SMS/USSD instructions, toolkit information, and support resources.
Present the application as safe, inclusive, privacy-conscious, and grounded in adolescent health needs.
Capture partner, school, clinic, donor, volunteer, and media enquiries through structured forms.
Support future scale by making the website multilingual, SEO-ready, analytics-enabled, and content-managed.

2. Product Context and Background
2.1 Application Summary
My Period Companion is designed as a digital and physical menstrual health support ecosystem. The application concept includes menstrual education, period tracking, rights and safety information, access to period products, emotional support, and referral pathways. It is designed for adolescent girls, especially first-time menstruators, while also supporting parents, educators, community health workers, NGOs, clinics, schools, policymakers, and funders.

2.3 Key Application Capabilities Referenced by the Website
Onboarding through QR code, WhatsApp START message, IVR, and other lightweight entry points.
Big Sister Mode for natural-language Q&A on periods, SRHR, consent, GBV, stigma, and referrals.
Period Tracker Lite that accepts voice, SMS, or WhatsApp entries and sends reminders.
Edutainment Hub with short drama or story-based learning content.
Toolkit Builder that helps users understand what to pack, how to care for products, and how to refill supplies.
Access and Locator Tool for clinics, vendors, free pads, and support services.

Accessibility layer covering text-to-speech, captions/transcripts, Braille-friendly print materials, and simple navigation.
2.4 Website Role in the Ecosystem
The website should act as the official public face of the application. It must communicate trust and clarity while avoiding overpromising clinical or emergency services. It should educate, route, capture, and report. The website should not store sensitive adolescent health data unless there is a clearly approved privacy, safeguarding, consent, and data governance process.

4. Website Scope
4.1 In-Scope for Version 1.0
Responsive official website for desktop, tablet, and mobile browsers.
Core pages: Home, About, How It Works, Features, First Period Toolkit, Learn, Accessibility and Inclusion, Partners, Impact, FAQ, Contact, Privacy and Safety.
Clear onboarding CTAs for WhatsApp, IVR, SMS/USSD, and QR code-based access.
Partner, school, clinic, media, and funder enquiry forms with notification workflow.
Content-managed blog/resource section for menstrual health articles, audio transcripts, and stories.
Basic analytics for traffic, CTA clicks, form submissions, downloads, resource engagement, and conversion routes.
SEO setup, social sharing metadata, privacy notice, cookie notice where applicable, and basic search engine indexing.
Accessibility requirements aligned with WCAG 2.2 AA and inclusive content principles.
Admin/CMS roles for content creation, review, publishing, and enquiry management.
4.2 Out-of-Scope for Version 1.0 Unless Approved
Full clinical case management system or electronic medical record.
Real-time emergency response or live counselling, unless delivered through approved partners.
Collection of detailed menstrual cycle histories directly on the public website.
Storage of sensitive child/adolescent health disclosures without approved safeguarding and consent protocols.
Full e-commerce marketplace for period products, unless a later phase adds vendor integrations.
Complex AI chatbot embedded on the website before safety, moderation, age-appropriate content, and escalation rules are fully approved.
Public map of sensitive support service locations if it creates safety, privacy, or stigma risks.
4.3 Assumptions
Assumption
Implication
The core application runs primarily through messaging, IVR, SMS/USSD, and physical toolkit pathways.

The target audience includes minors and first-time menstruators.

The project may work with schools, clinics, CSOs, pharmacies, and funders.

The product may expand across languages and West African contexts.
The architecture should support localization and content translation from the beginning.

10. Content Requirements
10.1 Content Principles
Use simple, warm, non-judgmental language that speaks to adolescents without sounding childish or clinical.
Avoid shame, stigma, fear-based messaging, or images that expose or embarrass girls.
Distinguish educational support from medical diagnosis, emergency response, or counselling.
Use culturally sensitive examples suitable for Nigeria and future West African expansion.
Provide clear referrals for urgent health concerns, abuse, violence, severe anxiety, or emergency situations.
Use plain language summaries before detailed explanations, especially for low-literacy audiences.
Ensure every audio or video resource has a transcript or caption alternative.

11. UI/UX and Visual Design Requirements
11.1 Design Direction
The website should feel safe, bright, youthful, respectful, and credible. The design should avoid overly clinical visuals while also avoiding childish stereotypes. It should balance warmth with professionalism so it can serve adolescents and institutional stakeholders at the same time.
Design Element
Requirement
Color palette
Use a friendly and trustworthy palette. Recommended direction: deep purple for trust, coral/pink for warmth, teal for freshness, and soft neutral backgrounds.
Typography
Use clear, legible sans-serif fonts with strong contrast and readable spacing. Avoid decorative fonts for body content.
Imagery
Use empowering, diverse, consented, culturally appropriate images of girls, caregivers, schools, community settings, and health workers. Avoid distressing or stigmatizing images.
Icons
Use simple icons to support low-literacy navigation: chat, phone, calendar, pad, school, clinic, shield, heart, location.
Tone
Warm, calm, reassuring, and practical. Use supportive phrasing such as “You are not alone” and “Here is how to start.”
Layout
Mobile-first, spacious, card-based sections, clear CTAs, short paragraphs, and visual cues.
Trust signals
Show partner logos, approved statements, testimonials, metrics, privacy commitments, and reviewer notes only when verified.

11.2 Mobile-First Requirements
Homepage must load quickly on low-bandwidth mobile connections.
Primary CTA buttons must be thumb-friendly and visible without excessive scrolling.
Navigation should collapse into a simple menu with Home, Start, Learn, Partners, and Contact prioritized.
Long resource content should use summaries, accordions, audio, and jump links.
Forms should be short, step-based where needed, and able to save or submit without unnecessary friction.
